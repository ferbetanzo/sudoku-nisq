    The Hamiltonian

    What is an operator: an operator in physics is a calculation rule that is applied on mathematical obects like numbers, functions, ...

    For example the multiplikation operator: Af(x):=2⋅f(x)Af(x) := 2 \cdot f(x)Af(x):=2⋅f(x)﻿

    or the derivation operator: ddxf(x):=f′(x)\frac{d}{dx}f(x) := f'(x)dxd​f(x):=f′(x)﻿

    Why operators: all observation variables (except time) are represented by hermetian operators in quantum physics. The possible measured values are Eigenvalues of the operators.

    Why Hamilton: the hamilton function in calssical mechanics is closely related to the total energy of ths system.

    The hamiltonian function becomes the hamiltonian operator via the correspondence principle and describes the energy in quantum mechanics.

    Derivation:

    In mechanics the total energy consists of kinetic and potential energy:

    Eges=Ekin+EpotE_{ges} = E_{kin} + E_{pot}Eges​=Ekin​+Epot​﻿

    Kinetic engery is calculated by:

    Ekin=12mv2E_{kin} = \frac{1}{2}mv^2Ekin​=21​mv2﻿

    In mechanics the state is not given by the speed but by location and momentum so we use:

    p=mvp = mvp=mv﻿

    So we get the new formula:

    Ekin=p22mE_{kin} = \frac{p^2}{2m}Ekin​=2mp2​﻿

    Potential energy in conservative force fields is described by a potential V(x). In specific non-conservative fields you can introduce a generalized potential V(x).

    Epot=V(x)E_{pot} = V(x)Epot​=V(x)﻿

    The total energy in mechanics consists of kinetic energy and potential energy:

    Etotal=p22m+V(x)E_{total} = \frac{p^2}{2m}+V(x)Etotal​=2mp2​+V(x)﻿

    Hamilton function:

    In conservative force fields the total energy is described by the hamilton function:

    H(x,p)=Etotal=p22m+V(x)H(x, p) = E_{total} = \frac{p^2}{2m} + V(x)H(x,p)=Etotal​=2mp2​+V(x)﻿

    Correspondence principle in quantum mechanics:

    Starting with the hamilton function that describes the total energy:

    H(x,p)=Etotal=p22m+V(x)H(x, p) = E_{total} = \frac{p^2}{2m} + V(x)H(x,p)=Etotal​=2mp2​+V(x)﻿

    Now we substitute all classical expressions by the corresponding quantum mechanical operators:

    Position operator: x^ψ(x)=x⋅ψ(x)\hat{x}\psi(x) = x \cdot \psi(x)x^ψ(x)=x⋅ψ(x)﻿ (multiplication operator)

    Momentum operator: p^ψ(x)=hi∂∂xψ(x)\hat{p}\psi(x) = \frac{h}{i} \frac{\partial}{\partial x} \psi(x)p^​ψ(x)=ih​∂x∂​ψ(x)﻿ (derivation operator)

    And get the hamilton operator:

    H^=−h22m∂2∂x2+V(x)\hat{H} = -\frac{h^2}{2m} \frac{\partial^2}{\partial x^2} + V(x)H^=−2mh2​∂x2∂2​+V(x)﻿

    What can you do with the hamilton operator:

    • it appears in the time dependent Schrödinger equation
    • it appears in the time independent Schrödinger equation

      H^ψ(x)=Eψ(x)\hat{H}\psi(x) = E\psi(x)H^ψ(x)=Eψ(x)﻿
